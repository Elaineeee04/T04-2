Televisions Energy Consumption

Understanding the Energy Rating Label

The Energy Rating Label is a mandatory label on most new appliances sold in Australia. For televisions, it helps you compare the energy efficiency of different models. The label shows two key pieces of information:

  • Star Rating: The more stars on the label, the more energy-efficient the television is. A higher star rating means lower running costs and a smaller environmental impact.
  • Energy Consumption: This number, measured in kilowatt-hours per year (kWh/year), indicates the estimated annual energy consumption. The lower the number, the better.

Always compare the energy consumption of televisions with a similar screen size, as larger TVs will naturally use more energy.

How to Calculate Your TV's Energy Cost

You can estimate your television's energy consumption and annual running cost using a simple formula. You will need your TV's estimated annual energy consumption from its Energy Rating Label (in kWh/year) and your electricity cost per kWh.

Estimated Annual Cost Formula:

Annual Cost ($) = (Annual Energy Consumption (kWh) × Electricity Cost (cents per kWh)) / 100

For example, if a TV's Energy Rating Label shows an annual consumption of 150 kWh and your electricity cost is 30 cents per kWh, the calculation would be:

(150 kWh × 30 cents) / 100 = $45

Your estimated annual running cost for that TV would be $45.

Tips to Reduce TV Energy Consumption

  • Adjust Brightness: Lowering the brightness of your TV can significantly reduce its energy use.
  • Use Sleep Timers: If you often fall asleep while watching, set a sleep timer to automatically turn the TV off.
  • Unplug or Use a Power Strip: Televisions consume a small amount of "standby" power even when turned off. Unplugging it or using a power strip you can switch off will eliminate this.
  • Enable Eco-Modes: Many modern TVs have "Eco" or "Energy Saving" modes that automatically adjust settings to optimize for lower energy consumption.
